According to a time-honored tradition in finance, it says that it's a promise to pay $1 , but it's not worth $1 today. It must worth less than $1.

#Expected utility theory

In Economics, it is assumed that people have a utility function, which represents how happy they are with an outcome.

If I have a monetary outcome, and I have a certain amount of money, x dollars. How happy I am with $x is called U(x).We have something called diminishing marginal utility.

Utility as the function of the amount of money I receive is downwardly-concave. The exact shape of the curve is subject to discussion, The point of diminishing marginal utility is that as you get more and more money, the increment in utility for each extra dollar diminishes. Usually we say it never goes down.

It's important--that's one reason why we want policies that encourage equality of incomes. Because the people with low wealth have a very marginal utility of income. And people with very high wealth have very little. So, if you take from the rich and give to the poor. You make people happier.

What finance theory and much of economics is based on the idea that want to maximize the expected utility of their wealth.
